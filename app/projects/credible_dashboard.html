credible offer dashboard redesign

Which offer should I choose?

overview

Credible is a marketplace for student loan refinancing. We partner with multiple lenders so that users can get their personalized rates without filling all these tedious application forms. I redesign the dashboard to help users make better decisions on choosing their loan options.

my role

Independently worked from conducting user research and creating low-fidelity wireframes to usability test and high-fidelity visual design.

the problem

which offer should I choose?

Credible is a marketplace for student loan refinancing. It helps borrowers get personalized rates from multiple lenders. It's great that users can receive multiple offers by providing data once. But the terms and benefits of each offer are very different. How to design the offer dashboard to help users find the best offer?

user research

how do people make choices?

In order to better meet users' needs, it's important to understand how do people make their choice among several options. I conducted user interviews to gather insights about the process of choosing a product and factors influence decision making.

"The refinancing offer should be better than my current loan"

Need current loan information for reference

For student loan refinancing users, they have very a clear goal that the refinancing offer should offer more benefit than their current loan. So users need their current loan information for reference.

"I can only pay up to $600 a month"

Filter the options with fixed criteria in mind

Before choosing the product, people usually have fixed criteria in mind. For example, their income situation will determine the maximize monthly payment. Some users are clear that they don't want to choose variable rate because they don't want to take the risk of APR getting higher.

"I can't tell what I want right now but I know what I don't"

Eliminate options that users are not interested in

For users who are shopping around, they really want to make sure they choose the best option. But it's quite difficult to find the best at once. Users prefer to eliminate options that they are not interested in and finally get to their favorite one.

Where does current loan information live?

The information architecture of the dashboard really affects how users process the information. So, where should current loan information live? I thought about the focus on the page structure first. I took these questions into consideration to make sure users can understand and navigate through dashboard easily.

  • Use tab structure for different loan type navigation
  • Put filter above offers instead of on the left side
  • Show new updates of the dashboard and current loan information on top

Improve the usability of filters

1Slider v.s. input

The sliders provide a clear visual cue of what it is and how to use it. However, adjusting the sliders to a specific number can be a difficult task. So I decide to keep the slider pattern but also give users the ability to type in they desired number directly.

2Checkbox, dropdown or radio

The old checkboxes for rate type can be confusing when both are unchecked. I explored checkboxes, radio buttons, drop down and pills, and decide on the current solution, which is easiest to understand and apply.

archive and compare offers

1Archive offers that users are not interested in

After filtering the offers with criteria, users can mark the offers as "not interested". Then the offer will be put into the archive category. If users change their mind, these offers can be put out of the archive.

2Compare options in viewport

One challenge here is providing detailed information about each offer, but also saving space of each offer. So that users can view and compare multiple offer details without scrolling up and down. I arranged information into sections and removed duplicated information with the filters to same move space.

usability testing

listen to user's voice and keep iterating

test overview

  • The participant found the overall page structure clear and easy to navigate.
  • The participant still had to think for a while about how to use "discount filter". He didn't understand what "apply discount" would do here before he applied it, nor what each option means before explanations.
  • The participant liked that he can archive offers he doesn't like, but he felt the "not interested" button call too much attention.

iteration

First, I changed the label "Apply discounts" to "APR discounts", so that users can understand what this filter is related to. Then I started to really think about users mental model - users would like to know possible discounts, then filter out if they don't meet requirements. So I decide to make the default to show both discounts, and users can un-check if they don't meet requirements.
